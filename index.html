Methodology & data sources
What the Grid Stress Index (GSI) is

GSI is a modeled 0–100 composite built for strategic prioritization — it is not an official Con Edison operational metric. Con Edison does not publish exact substation or feeder-level locations or loading (reasonably, for security reasons), so this tool blends a real public health dataset with publicly documented, borough-level characteristics of Con Edison's grid architecture:

GSI = 0.5 × Heat Risk + 0.3 × Outage Exposure + 0.2 × (100 − Redundancy)

Heat Risk

Taken directly from the NYC Department of Health & Mental Hygiene's Heat Vulnerability Index (1–5 scale per ZCTA), which summarizes surface temperature, green space, home A/C access, and socio-economic risk factors, rescaled to 0–100.

Outage Exposure & Redundancy

Illustrative borough-level estimates derived from Con Edison's public descriptions of its two grid types: dense, mostly-underground network grids (high redundancy — a feeder can be pulled for maintenance with no customer impact) concentrated in Manhattan and inner Brooklyn/Bronx, versus radial "auto-loop" grids (single/paired feeders, more overhead cable, more storm- and heat-fault exposure) more common in Staten Island and outer Queens. These are directional estimates, not per-feeder measurements.

Recommended strategies

Each node's recommendations are generated by rules that mirror interventions Con Edison has publicly reported pursuing: AMI/machine-learning "replace-before-failure" feeder maintenance, submersible fault interrupters, conservation voltage optimization, feeder undergrounding, and demand-response/peak-shaving — matched to whichever combination of heat risk, outage exposure, and redundancy a zone actually has.

Extreme-Weather Grid Vulnerability — TX · CA · NY

Texas, California, and New York face three structurally different threats to grid reliability — isolation during winter cold, wildfire ignition risk, and dense-urban heat load — shaped as much by grid architecture and interconnection as by weather itself.

Methodology & data sources — state comparison
What the Hazard Exposure Index is

A 0–100 illustrative score per hazard axis, built from each grid's publicly documented structural characteristics and event history — not a formal risk model from ERCOT, CAISO, or NYISO. It's meant to make the differences in kind of vulnerability visible, not to rank states by overall risk.

Why interconnection matters so much

Texas's ERCOT is the only grid in the continental U.S. not substantially tied to the Eastern or Western Interconnection, so it cannot import power from neighboring states in an emergency — a major reason Winter Storm Uri's 2021 rolling blackouts happened at all. California's CAISO and New York's NYISO both sit inside interconnected grids, which adds import capacity during local shortfalls but also carries cascading-failure risk, as in the August 2003 Northeast blackout.
